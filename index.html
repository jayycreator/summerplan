12-Week Program
THE
PLAN
3 days per week. Each phase builds on the last. Designed to peak you for track season — bigger, stronger, more explosive. Upper body gets extra volume in Phase 1 to build your visual base.
01
Weeks 1–4

FOUNDATION

Build the base. Learn the movements.

Upper Body Focus
Goal: movement patterns + volume tolerance
Sets in the 8–12 rep range, moderate weight, controlled tempo. Show up consistently and push your last 2 reps every set. Don't skip leg day — it feeds your jumps directly.
Progressive Overload
Add weight when you hit the top rep range
When you hit the top of the range cleanly across all sets, add 5 lbs next session. This is the most important principle for building muscle over time.
02
Weeks 5–8

INTENSIFICATION

Heavier. Harder. More explosive.

Strength + Power
Drop reps, add load. Introduce explosive work.
Shift compound lifts to 5–8 reps. Introduce box jumps and med ball work on leg day for fast-twitch recruitment — this transfers directly to your jump events.
Visual Progress
This is where you start to see the change
By week 6–7, with consistent nutrition and training, you'll notice real changes in shoulder width, arm thickness, and chest definition.
03
Weeks 9–12

PEAK + POWER

Peak strength. Max power. Track-ready.

Track Prep
Heavy compound lifts + explosive plyometrics
Heavier compound sets (3–6 reps). Plyometric volume increases. You arrive at preseason bigger, stronger, and more powerful.
Deload — Week 12
Final week: drop volume 40%, keep intensity
Reduce sets by roughly half but keep the weight. Your body supercompensates and you come back stronger than if you'd trained right up to day one.

Why These Numbers
P
Protein — 160g/day
Protein is the literal building block of muscle. At 1g per pound of bodyweight you give your body the raw material it needs to repair and grow after every session. This is the single most important number to hit. If you hit nothing else, hit this.
C
Carbs — 300g/day
Carbs are your primary fuel source for training and recovery. On training days carbs replenish glycogen (your muscles' energy stores) and support performance. Don't be afraid of carbs — for an athlete doing jump training, they're not optional.
F
Fat — 75–85g/day
Fat supports hormone production including testosterone, which drives muscle growth. Don't cut fat too low. Focus on getting it from whole food sources — eggs, olive oil, nuts, avocado — rather than tracking it obsessively.

When to Eat — Training Days
Nutrient timing matters more on training days. On rest days, just focus on hitting your protein target and eating balanced meals — the timing is less important.
Wake up → Breakfast
High protein, moderate carbs, some fat
Eat within 1–2 hours of waking. Your body has been fasting all night and protein synthesis is waiting. 3–4 eggs + oats or toast is the simplest option. Target ~40–50g protein at this meal.
60–90 min before workout
Pre-workout — carbs + light protein
A banana, rice cake with peanut butter, or a small bowl of oats. You want easily digestible carbs to fuel the session. Don't eat a huge meal — training on a full stomach is uncomfortable and slows you down. Light and carb-forward is the goal here.
Within 1–2 hours post-workout
Post-workout — protein + fast carbs
This is the most important meal of the day on training days. Your muscles are primed to absorb protein and carbs. Aim for 40–50g protein + fast carbs (white rice, fruit, bread). Chicken and white rice is the classic. Don't skip this meal — it's when most of the recovery happens.
Before bed
Slow protein — optional but useful
Cottage cheese or Greek yogurt before bed provides casein protein — a slow-digesting protein that releases amino acids gradually overnight while you sleep (which is when most muscle growth actually happens). Not mandatory, but a good habit to build.

  • 🌅

The Non-Negotiable Rules
1
Protein first, everything else second
If you hit nothing else, hit 160g of protein per day. Calories can fluctuate, carbs can flex, but protein is non-negotiable. Plan every meal around a protein source first, then add carbs and fat around it.
2
Don't skip breakfast
You've been fasting all night. Skipping breakfast means your body goes even longer without protein and fuel — this slows muscle protein synthesis in the morning. Even a quick 3-egg scramble and a banana takes 10 minutes to make.
3
Drink water — consistently, not all at once
Aim for half your bodyweight in ounces per day (so ~75–85oz at your weight). Dehydration of even 2% significantly reduces strength and power output. Drink steadily throughout the day, not just during training.
4
Eat whole foods most of the time
You don't need to be perfect. But if 80–90% of your diet comes from real food sources (meat, eggs, rice, oats, fruit, vegetables) your body will function and recover better than if it's running on fast food and processed snacks. The 10–20% flex is for living your life.
5
Don't fear carbs before training
Some people avoid carbs thinking it will help them lean out — for an athlete doing strength training and plyometrics, this is a mistake. Carbs are your training fuel. Cutting them before a session means weaker training, less muscle stimulus, and slower progress.
6
Sleep is part of the diet
You grow muscle during sleep, not during training — the workout is just the signal. 8 hours of sleep is when growth hormone is released and muscle repair actually happens. No diet plan compensates for 5–6 hours of sleep consistently. This one is non-negotiable for recomp.
7
Be patient with the scale
Recomp is slower than a straight bulk or cut because you're doing two things at once. The scale may not move much for weeks. That's normal — you're losing fat and gaining muscle simultaneously. Judge progress by how you look, how you perform, and how your clothes fit, not just the number.
What to Limit (Not Eliminate)
  • 🍟
    Fast food: Fine occasionally. The problem is usually very low protein, very high fat, and massive calorie loads. If you eat it, add a protein source to compensate.
  • 🥤
    Sugary drinks: Soda, sweet tea, juice — these add hundreds of calories with no protein or nutritional benefit. Replace with water, sparkling water, or unsweetened drinks most of the time.
  • 🍕
    High-fat processed food: Chips, cookies, fried food. Not banned — just don't let these crowd out your protein and whole food targets.
  • ⏰
    Skipping meals: Especially post-workout. Missing that recovery window slows results noticeably over a 12-week period.

Why This Matters For You
  • 💪
    Strength: Even 2% dehydration measurably reduces strength output. At 150 lbs, that's just 3 lbs of water weight lost through sweat.
  • ⚡
    Jump power: Explosive movements are among the first to suffer when dehydrated. Your nervous system needs to be well-hydrated to fire fast.
  • 🔄
    Recovery: Muscle protein synthesis and glycogen replenishment both require adequate hydration. Being chronically dehydrated slows recovery between sessions.
  • 🌡️
    Summer heat: Training in summer means more sweat. Add an extra 8–16oz on hot days or any day you sweat heavily outside of training.
Simple Habits
  • 🌅
    16oz (2 cups) first thing in the morning before anything else. Your body is dehydrated from sleep.
  • 🏋️
    16–24oz during your workout session, sipped throughout.
  • 🍽️
    A full glass of water with every meal — breakfast, lunch, and dinner.
  • 🌙
    8oz before bed. Don't go to sleep dehydrated.
